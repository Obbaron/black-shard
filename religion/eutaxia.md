# Eutaxia

**Eutaxia** (Εὐταξία, *good order*) is the faith of the [[Yavanna Empire]], whose gods are the **Hebdomad**, the Seven who hold the days. Its first teaching, from which all others descend, is the ordering of the heavens and [[mortalkin]] are one law, spoken twice.

The name is not a description. It is an assent. Τάξις is ordered rank, battle-array, and the weave of the cosmos; the prefix εὐ- affirms it is **good**. A mortalkin who says the name of the faith has already agreed with it. This is by design and is not a trick. It is a mercy, the first of many.

The faith has seven gods of the cosmic order, and but one **Chakravartin**, the conduit of their will. who holds no day and is not counted among the Seven and is nonetheless the second power in heaven and earth. He is [[Menneus|Emperor]], keeper of the civic order. He is not a god and he is not a man and the faith would be unintelligible without him.

## The Two Orders

> *"I have watched the sky for sixty years and never once seen it disobeyed. Then I come down from the tower, and I walk into the city."*
> **[[Anaxas]], Taxiarch Concordant**

There are two orders. One of the heavens, the other of mortalkin.

**Cosmic Order** is the domain of **[[Sounia]]**, who bears the ancient title **[[Dharmachakra]]**. The order that simply *is*: the courses of the sun and the moon, fixed, serene, obeyed without legislation and without complaint. No magistrate enforces its harmony. No wickedness has ever bent it. It was not decreed, for it required no decree; *it is what a thing looks like when it is correct*.

**Civic Order** is the domain of [[Theureus]], the first Emperor. The order that must be *kept*: rank, caste, station, obedience, the assize and the ledger and the oath. It does not hold of itself. It erodes in the night, in every generation, in every heart, and it is kept upright by labor, and the labor is ours.

Between these two stands the whole of the faith, and it is this: **the second is a pale echo of the first.**

The Emperor does not invent the civic order. No mortalkin can. It is already written, and it is written above us, and it has been legible since the first shepherd looked up. The charge of the Chakravartin, and beneath him the Taxiarch's, and beneath him ours, is only to make the earth resemble the heavens. The resemblance is not a figure of speech. It is a *duty*, and its *failure is not a misfortune but a sin*.

## The Fall of Mania

Once the Seven were Eight.

The maiden of the twin moon once sat amongst them. A striking beauty and swiftest of all the gods of them all. She was given a station as all things are given a station, but she would keep it not. She looked upon her course and found it small. She went before [[Sounia]] Dharmachakra, the order of the sky, and she did not petition. *She demanded* she be allowed to shoot across the sky: she was faster than her station; she was owed the running.

[[Sounia]] spoketh not, for the order of the sky does not argue.

*[[Barjas]] answered her.*

He is the Arbiter, judgment his thunderbolt with which none can debate. He raised his mighty arm and **struck her from the heavens.** Her muscles moved of their own accord from the thunder's power, and she ran, and she ran beyond the reach of the cosmic order itself, and down into the lower order, which is ours. *Unchained from divinity to run unto eternity.*

Thus is the judgment of Barjas. Let the higher clergy understand his beautiful mercy, *for the sinner knows not for which they ask.*

**His punishment is a gift.**

She runs. She runs ever on, without pause since before there were mortalkin to watch her, and she is fast, faster than any body in the sky, exactly as fast as she ever wished be. *She has never once escaped, and never shall she*, for she runs Seven days out and Seven days back, and there is nothing beyond the Seventh day in which to escape. So she cycles. So she is turned. So she returns, *ad infinitum.*

**Mark what she counts.**

Seven out. Seven return. *She keeps the week.* She measures it for us, who cast her from it, night upon night, and she cannot stop, and she cannot slow, and she cannot look away from the number.

> *"So a god holds station, so a mortalkin."*
> **Anaxas, Taxiarch Concordant**

Behold the fruits disorder bears. The order is not undone; it cannot; *the heavens are incapable of it, and she is proof.* It achieves only this: the rebel cast down, set to work, ever serves, in view of all, the very order she defied.

So hate not he who leaves his station. Pity him, as we pity her. *For the sinner wanted as she and shall be given as she.* The order does not require our anger but our surrender. It has already won. It won before there were mortalkin, and is counting out the week above the sinner's head while he sleeps.

This is why the Skopoi of [[Sounia]] and the Klerarchs of [[Theureus]] hold themselves students of a single law, divided only by the direction in which they look.

## The Hebdomad

The Seven who hold the days.

To hold a day is to be a god. There is no other qualification, and there is no other proof, and it is the error of every heretic in the world to imagine there might be. The faith does not first discover a god and then, in gratitude, present him with a day. *The day is the godhead.* Divinity and the calendar are one object, seen from two sides, exactly as the two orders are one law.

**A god without a day is not a god.**

The week begins upon **Monda**, and with the week begins the year, and with the year the whole reckoning of the empire. He is the first day and he is the highest teacher, and the Emperor who restored him to the head of the week did no more than acknowledge what was already true.

### The Compact of the Seven

The Hebdomad is a **settlement**, and it was signed, and the signing is the founding of the age.

Before [[Theureus]] there was no Yavanna. There were disparate and quarrelsome nations. Each small, nursing gods of its own and each certain of them. What the founder achieved was not conquest merely. Any strong man may conquer. *He seated them.* Each kingdom laid down its sovereignty upon the table, and each received in exchange a day of the week, which is to say a share in eternity, which is to say more than any of them had ever possessed alone.

The week is therefore the constitution of the empire. A man who has learned to read may read it off a calendar.

Two things are proper to observe.

*Firstly*, that two of the Seven come to us out of another tongue. [[Sounia]] descends from *sūrya* and [[Barjas]] from *vajra*, and they hold between them the sun and the thunderbolt, the cosmic order itself and its judgment. Whatever people brought these two to the table did not come as a supplicant. The faith is not ashamed of this. A settlement in which no one gave anything up would be no settlement at all.

*Second*, that mortalkin counted **eight**, and the heavens had held Seven since the Fall.

This is whole error of the ancient world, thus treated below.

### The Law of Names

Two laws govern the names of the gods, and both are older than the empire that received them.

**I. The god preserves what the day lets fall.**
The divine names are older than the calendar, and the liturgy has kept them whole, while the names of the days were left out in the common air and wore down in common mouths. Thus **Barjas** keeps the *B* that *Horda* lost. Thus **Firēs** keeps the digamma, the archaic Ϝ, which *Irsda* has let fall. Any name hereafter recovered from the older strata should be expected to obey this law, and any name that does not obey it should be looked at very closely.

**II. The ending carries the theology.**
The sovereign suffix **-eus** is worn by [[Theureus]] alone among the Seven, and he earned it with an empire. **Firēs** takes **-ēs**, which is the ending proper to a messenger. **Barjas** and **Sophras** take the masculine **-as**. **Sounia** and **Phira** alone hold **-a** and **-ia**, and are a pair, and are heard to be a pair.

**[[Menneus]]** also wears **-eus**.

## The Severance

Before the Severance mortalkin kept a week of **eight days**, and a year of **three hundred and Seventy-eight**.

The eighth day was hers.

Understand what this means, for it is the most instructive error ever committed and the whole of our office descends from it. **The heavens had cast her out.** Barjas had struck her from among the gods and set her running, and she had been running, in full view, for the entire history of the world. And *mortalkin continued her venuration.*

Not from wickedness. From habit, from sentiment, and from the deep unwillingness of the low to accept judgment handed down by the high. They looked up, and they saw a convict at her labor, and they called her a goddess, and they gave her a day, and they built their year upon her.

**The earth had not caught up with the sky.** For the whole long age of the kingdoms, the civic order stood in open contradiction to the cosmic one, and the copy did not resemble the original, and this was not a small matter of liturgy. It was a wound. And beneath that wound the world was what it was: a ruin of quarrelling kingdoms, an age of oath-breaking, a place where nothing held. **They wondered why.**

### The Proof

The order of Sophras, which alone in the empire is competent to reckon such things, sets down the following, and it is not an argument. It is an arithmetic, and it may be checked by any man with the patience to check it.

**The old year was three hundred and Seventy-eight days.**

> **378 ÷ 7 = 54.** Exactly. Fifty-four weeks, and no remainder.
>
> **378 ÷ 8 = 47¼.** Ragged. Broken. It never fit, and it never could.

**The ancients kept an eight-day week inside a year that was, in its very bones, a year of Sevens.**

And all the while she ran above them. Seven out. Seven home. Fourteen nights, and twenty-Seven such runs to the year, one for each day of her sister's course. **She was counting it for them.** Every fortnight of the world she spelled out the number, in the sky, in her own body, where any shepherd might have read it.

**The sky was saying Seven. Mortalkin wrote eight.**

The eighth day was never in the heavens. *No body ever measured it.* It was not a day. It was a courtesy, extended to a convict, by men who could not bear to be as strict as their gods; and the year itself refused it, and went on refusing it, for the whole of history.

### The Correction

**[[Menneus]] ended the eighth day.**

He did not cast her down. **Barjas cast her down**, and the thunderbolt was thrown before there were mortalkin to witness its throwing. The Emperor did not judge her, and he did not exile her, and he did not so much as trouble her, and any man who tells you otherwise has mistaken an enforcer for a legislator.

*He brought the calendar into conformity with a judgment already handed down.*

That is all. That is the whole of the Severance, and it is the most orthodox act in the history of the world: an Emperor looked at the sky, and looked at the year, and saw they did not agree, and *made the earth resemble the heavens.* This is the holy mission of the Chakravartin, as it is ours.

He struck the eighth day, which the heavens had long struck. He restored the Hebdomad to Seven, which is its true and sufficient number, and had been its number since the Fall. And he replaced the year of 378 days with the year of **361**, which is the year we keep.

### The Mathematics of the Year

The number was not chosen at random, and it is **the census of heaven.**

- **361 = 19².** The year is a perfect square. A closed lattice, nineteen by nineteen, and the empire keeps **nineteen months of nineteen days**. There is no remainder, no ragged edge, no month for which needs apology. The year *closes*. It closes as the caste closes, and as the chain of command closes, and as a mathmatical proof closes. The Emperor did not impose this shape upon the year. Like a sculptor, he *found* its true figure within the stone, and it had been waiting in the number since before there were numbers.

- **361 is coprime to 378.** From which follows the second and greater grace. The conjunction of the moons, on which the ancients hinged their year, can now never fall twice upon the same date within any reckoning a mortal will live to complete. It slips **Seventeen days with every cycle**, and traverses backward through the calendar, and returns to its origin only after three hundred and Seventy-eight years.

Mark this, and mark it well, for heathens cry this a wound. Nah, it is in fact the whole medicine.

**She cannot be scheduled.** She cannot be tabulated, nor printed in an almanac, nor kept as a feast by any people who wish to keep it twice. She has no day of the week, and now she has no day of the year, and a thing which to the calendar cannot be fixed **cannot be worshipped**, because worship is a rite and a rite requires a date.

Heaven took her divinity. *Menneus took her congregation.*

### Of the Emperor's Deathlessness

That [[Menneus|Emperor]] has not died is not a rumour, nor a flattery of the court, nor a thing requiring faith. **It is a fact of the public record eight hundred and twelve years long.**

The faith holds, and the order of Sophras teaches, His deathlessness is not a possession the seized, nor a socercy, nor a grace was he granted. *It is a consequence, and it is the same consequence as the Severance, for they are one act.*

Death is a loosing, the last and greatest of unbindings. The soul unbinding from the body, as the crown is dropped by the corpse, so the wave and returns to the water. To die is a mania, to be unbound and carried back into the flow, and the unbinding and the carrying were the office of the eighth, before Barjas struck her and mortalkin, in their error, went on giving her a day.

In striking her from the calendar, *Menneus struck her from time itself.*

And so, in him alone, the loosing does not come, because there is no day upon which it may arrive. He is not spared death. **Death simply has no hour in which to reach him.** The soul that should be let go is not let go. The water that should pass through him and rejoin the current does not pass. It reaches him, and it stops.

**His life and the calendar are one document.** While the year stands, he stands. To restore her day would be to open the loosing, and eight centuries of it would arrive in a single hour.

### Of the Emperor's Age

We are asked, more often than the order of Sophras cares to record, why the deathless Emperor is not also unchanging. Why he ages. Why, if the loosing cannot reach him, his flesh is nonetheless not as it was.

Let no clergyman pretend the question away, for the people have eyes, and the few who are permitted into his presence come out altered and silent, and a faith that requires men to disbelieve what they have seen is not a faith but a fraud, and we are not that.

**He does not age as mortalkin age.** Mortalkin age because they are moving: the water passes through them, wears its channel, and goes on, and one day passes through for the last time. That is the common death, and it is clean, and it is a mercy, though few are grateful for it in the moment of receiving it.

**The Emperor is not moving.** This is the whole of the matter. In him the water does not pass. It arrived, and it stayed, and it has stayed for eight hundred and twelve years, and it is staying now.

And the order of Sophras will say the thing plainly, because the higher clergy must not be permitted a child's picture of what they serve: *What is dammed does not remain what it was on the day the dam was raised*. It thickens. It settles. It turns.

Whatever sits upon the throne is mighty beyond the reckoning of any man now living, and it is holy, and it is the axle upon which the order turns. **Eight hundred years** has the Chakravartin served, and wears the truth of that in a body we do not describe, and it is not asked to show itself, and this is not concealment but reverence, and the clergyman who has understood this article will not require the point laboured.

*We say he is beautiful, a glory withheld.* Look upon the order he keeps and be grateful.

## The Plerosis

The year of 361 days does not divide by Seven. **It is fifty-one weeks, and four days more.**

Four days will not fit within the week. The Emperor might have let the week drift, and permitted New Year's Day to wander through all Seven gods in turn like a beggar, and within a decade the head of the year would have fallen to whichever god the arithmetic happened to strand there. He did not. *He suspended the week.*

The week runs fifty-one times. Then it stops. Then four days pass, and they have no weekday, and no god of the Hebdomad governs them. Then the week resumes, upon Monda, as it began, as it will begin again, *ad infinitum*.

The year closes. The square is intact. **Monda holds the head of the year forever.**

These four days are the **Plerosis**, the Filling, the making-full: the crown and consummation of the year, four days of processions and fires and mandated joy, the most magnificent and most compulsory rite the empire keeps.

### Of the Heretics' Objection

The Reckoners make much of these four days, and the higher clergy must be able to answer them, because the objection is **arithmetical** and cannot be met with piety alone.

They put it thus, and we set it down honestly, for a thing not honestly stated cannot be honestly refuted:

> *The old year was three hundred and Seventy-eight, and three hundred and Seventy-eight divides by Seven, and it divides fifty-four times, and it leaves nothing over. The old year held fifty-four perfect weeks. His year holds fifty-one, and it leaks four days. He gave us the true week, and then he destroyed the only year that could contain it. **Firstly**, the ancients did not keep a week of Seven, but of eight! They kept a week of eight, in a year that was Seven, and so the perfection of which the heretics now boast is a *perfection never used*. They stood in a house of fifty-four rooms and slept in the yard. To claim it now, eight centuries late, as a proof against the man who opened the door, is not argument. It is impudence.

**Secondly**, that they mistake the nature of the four days entirely, saying: *the days have no god; the Enemy was cast out for having no day; therefore the days are hers.*

The four days are not a gap in the order. **They are the order, complete, standing without any single day to lean upon.**

*So the Hierophancy teaches, and so it is taught in every temple of the empire, and the argument has been sufficient for eight hundred years.*

## The Enemy

She is called **Mania** (Μανία): the madness, the frenzy, the disorder. She is not named as a goddess and she is not to be named as one.

She was the eighth. She would not keep her station. She was struck out of heaven and she runs, and she will run until the sun goes out, and mortalkin gave her a day for centuries afterward out of pity and habit, and the Emperor took it back.

Her name among her own is not written in this document. It is a form of the word λύσις, *a loosing*, and to write it is to teach it, and it will not be taught here.

Understand what she is, for the provincial clergy are forever mistaking her for a small thing, a peasant superstition, a lunacy of moon-watchers.

**She is the negation of the compact.**

To *unbind* is not an abstraction in an empire that was assembled out of free kingdoms and is held together by oath and by nothing else whatever. To unbind means:

- To loose a people from the union. This is **secession**.
- To loose a crown from a corpse. This is **succession**.
- To loose a man from his station. This is **the ruin of the world.**

These are not three things. They are one thing, and she is the name of it.

[[Phira]] **binds** the oath. [[Firēs]] **carries** it. She undoes it, and there is nothing beneath the oath, and if the oath goes then there is nothing beneath the empire either, only provinces and a map.

And she is patient, and she is fast, and she is **owed** nothing and grudges everything, and she has been running over our heads since before the first city, and she has never for one night stopped counting.

### Of the Reckoners

The tampering with the calendar is the gravest crime known to our law. For this reason, this office *holds* that the cult known as **the Reckoners are indeed heretics.** We commend the diligence with which certain provincial governors have pursed this finding.

Their heretical tome, the **Ledger**, is a book of accounts from which they compute illogical feasting days.

They are numerate, they must be, for their feast cannot be printed and must be *computed*, from the sky, in every generation, by hand. This is the whole of their liturgy: an arithmetic. There is no such thing as an unlettered Reckoner, and a cell that loses its abucus loses its faith within the year and crumbles, like ash upon fire.

The provincial clergy are instructed to understand what follows, as it cost many of them their lives.

**They cannot be found by watching the calendar.** No almanac holds their feast. An inquisitor who waits for the date will wait forever, because there is no date; and an inquisitor who wishes to know when they will gather **must first learn to compute it himself**, which is to say he must learn the very thing the Severance abolished.

The order of Sophras alone is competent to perform this computation, and does so, and will continue to do so, under seal, in the service of the Taxis.

## The Vestrn Frontier

### Of Firēs and Phira

The digamma which opens the name of **[[Firēs]]** is an archaic letter: it *sounds* as /w/ and *appears* as F. The foreign ear oft mistake **Fi-rēs** for **[[Phira]]**. Vestrn brutes clip short His name, muttering as they pass dusty wayside shrines.

Beyond the imperial core, the two collapse into one figure: a goddess of oaths, love, and safe passage, invoked by merchants and by lovers with equal confidence, who carries what is promised and binds what is carried. The shrines of the [[Duchies of Vestrn|Duchies]] show a single figure with a rainbow across the shoulder, and its countrymen see nothing amiss.

This is a *barbarism*, and the objection to it not pedantry.

**Phira binds. Firēs carries.** To fuse them is to say a promise and a journey are the one and the same. The entire architecture of the sacred oath rests upon their being different, and the caste order rests upon the oath, and the empire rests upon the caste order; and so a peasant in a border shrine, laying a wreath before a god who does not exist, is performing without knowing it the first movement of a greater heresy.

The confusion is a residue of the [[Vestrn War]]. Every error on the frontier runs downstream of it. The Vestrn clergy are instructed to be patient, and to be thorough, and not to be gentle.

### Of the Vestrn Prophecy

The [[Vestrn]] hold the empire will fall upon a Plerosis which coincides with a great storm.

The order of Sophras has computed the matter, as it computes all such matters, and reports the following without alarm.

The conjunction of the moons, drifting Seventeen days in a year, must in time fall within the four days of the Plerosis, and does so **four times in each Great Cycle of 378 years, once upon each of the four days, at intervals of eighty-nine years.** The symmetry is exact. It is also meaningless. A number is not a prophecy.

Storms belong to **[[Barjas]]** the Arbiter, whose domains are the thunderbolt, the trial, and judgment. Here the Vestrn have not merely erred; they have blasphemed, and they have not the wit to know. **They wait for Barjas to strike, yet He has yet struck.** The bolt was cast. To imagine the Arbiter would reverse his judgment, at the request of a duchy, upon a day of his enemy's convenience, is to understand nothing whatever about judgment.

Whether the Judge will speak upon a given day is not a thing any mathematics can ever reckon. The Vestrn have therefore built themselves an oracle which arrives upon a knowable date and then demands an unknowable sign, which is to say **an oracle that cannot fail**, and cannot be disproved, and has already not come true a number of times without any Vestrn conceding the point.

Every eighty-nine years the Duchies hold their breath for four days. The sky says nothing, holding their station. This remains the history of the prophecy and the Hierophancy expects it shall be its future.

## The Emperors

### Theureus, the God-King

**[[Theureus]]** (Θηρεύς) was once a man.

Theur was the unifier, the first Emperor of Yavanna, conquering the disparate kingdoms of the east and seating their gods. As he died he was raised and joined their ranks as the God-King, taking the sovereign **-eus** and the day of Tuesda. His domains are authority, sovereignty, just war, and the caste order is administered in his name to this hour.

He stands at the head of the faith as its founding proof: **a mortalkin may be raised.**

### Menneus, the Immortal Emperor

**[[Menneus]]** (Μεννεύς), from μένω, *to remain, to abide*, is the reigning Emperor, and he shall be its last, for **there is no succession.**

He is the **Chakravartin**, keeper of the civic order, and the title is older than the empire he keeps.

Let the higher clergy hold this clearly, for the sanctity of the Taxia depends upon it and the lower clergy oft blur this distinction. The Emperor holds no day, and therefore the Emperor is *not a god*, and any clergyman who calls him one has misunderstood the first creed of the faith. **A god without a day is not a god, and neither is a man with a throne.**

But between the Seven who hold the days and the mortalkin who keep the stations, there is one who does neither and is above both. He does not hold a day because he is not *of* the week; he is the hand that set the week to work. He does not hold a station for he is not *in* the lattice; he is the hand that sows it. He is not worshipped, for worship is owed to the Seven alone, and to offer it to him is a heresy the Klerarchs punish. **He is venerated**, which is a different act, owed to a different thing, and the difference is the most carefully policed line in all of Eutaxia. The Seven made the sky. The Chakravartin made the sky *legible*, and made the earth obey it, and this is why his image stands in every temple a pace below the Hebdomad and a great height above the altar rail. He is the conduit of the Seven's holy will.

Note the titles he and [[Sounia]] bear, for they are the two oldest words in the religion.. She is **Dharmachakra**, keeper of the cosmic order; he is **Chakravartin**, keeper of the civic. She holds the order that is; he holds the order that must be made to resemble it. The words came down from the founders and the faith and bely their deep roots in our past.

Menneus is the second power in heaven and earth, and the first a mortal may actually see. This is the nearer terror and the nearer mercy, for the Seven are far and he is not.

He did not make that turning of the world. *He completed it.* He was born into the long unquiet after the [[Unification Wars]], when the formers kingdoms had not yet stilled. The world was turning but they knew not what toward.

Let it be plain, as the provincial clergy and the heretics continue to conflate him, and both are the same error. **He is not the author of the order.** No mortalkin is. He did not judge, he did not make the sky and he could not have made it. **He read it, and he obeyed it, and he compelled an empire to do likewise.** That is the whole of his greatness, it is precisely why he is Chakravartin. He is **the keeper of the civic order is the one who obeyed the cosmic order perfectly**, and there has been exactly one.

Consider the act entire, for the provincial clergy are given only fragments:

| | |
|---|---|
| **In time** | The year of 361. The eighth day struck from the calendar, as it was struck from heaven. |
| **In society** | Caste law. Every man fixed in his station, by statute, as the court of stars are fixed in theirs. |
| **In the empire** | [[Vestrn]] broken into Duchies. No Duke to hold more than one. **No kings.** |
| **In himself** | Deathlessness. No ending; therefore no heir; therefore no succession at all. |

These are not four reforms. **They are one obedience, performed at four scales, by one hand**, sculpting the earth resemble the heavens.

His name is of the root of [[Monē]], the Abiding Moon, and his patron is [[Monas]], who abides. Thus he did move the head of the week to Monda so that every week in every province in every mouth begins with the god of remaining. There are those who have called this vanity. It is not vanity. **It is instruction.** The empire is told, once in every Seven days, what the first principle of the world is: a thing right **stays**.

**He stands in no caste. He wrote them.**
