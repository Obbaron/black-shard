# The Imperial Reckoning
### That a Vestrn eye may read

You have come because the reeve now writes every debt and every court-day in the Empire's count, and a man who cannot read the calendar of them that rule him is a man who will be cheated of a season and never know it. So sit, and learn it as I learned it. I will give you their year whole, that you may keep the days.

Our forefathers kept a plainer count, a kindlier one, in the years when Lysara's name was said aloud over the fields and the dragons still slumbered under the fells. The Empire took it all from us with the crown: the count, the goddess, and the long red sleep beneath the hills. What you learn here you learn because you must.

---

## The Whole of It, on One Slate

Here I have drawn the whole year. Keep this leaf and read the rest off it when your memory fails. First the shape of the year; then the seven days; then the nineteen months in their ranks. Learn these three marks and you need never be cheated of a season.

**The shape of the year.** Nineteen days down, nineteen months across: a perfect square. Yet the four borrowed days will not fit inside it, and hang off the end, belonging to no month nor day.

## The Shape of the Year

Their year is **three hundred and sixty-one days**. Learn the number and why they love it so: it is nineteen laid upon nineteen, nineteen times over; for the nineteen heavinly bodies they keep. The Yona hold a whole thing to be a holy thing, and a square to be the wholest, and so their year shuts like a box with the lid true.

Out of those days they make **nineteen months**, and each month is **nineteen days**. Nineteen and nineteen, no more, no less, the same nineteen down the whole year. This is easier than our old moons, I will grant them, for once you have learned one month you have learned them all, and no month is longer or shorter than his brother.

Over the months they lay a **week of seven days**, and here is the first thing you must hold fast: **the week begins on Monda**, always, and the year begins on Monda, always, and so long as the Empire stands the first light of the first month will be a Monda and nothing else. They have nailed it down, as is their way.

Now, seven will not go into their square. Fifty-one weeks fill the year and there are **four days left standing**, belonging to no day nor month.

---

## The Seven Days

Each day is a god of theirs. The day *is* the god, and the god is the day; they make no distingution between the two. Learn the god and you have learned the day.

| The day | The god it is | What it holds |
|---|---|---|
| **Monda** | Monas | the moon, and stillness, and being alone |
| **Tuesda** | Theureus | the God-King, lordship, rightful war |
| **Wirsda** | Firēs | the wind, the sky, the road, the message |
| **Horda** | Barjas | the storm, the bolt, the judgment |
| **Frida** | Phira | love, the wedding, the sworn oath |
| **Sorda** | Sophras | wisdom, the old learning, the reckoning of time |
| **Sunda** | Sounia | the sun, the fire, the order of heaven |

Three of them you will never forget, for the god is plain in the day: Monda is the moon's, and Sunda the sun's, and Frida is for love and for the swearing of oaths, and it is on Frida still our folk are wed. The other four you may hang upon those three like coats upon three pegs.

## The Nineteen Months

Here is the long labor, and I will not pretend it is light. The Empire names its months in ranks, as a captain names his lines, and if you learn the ranks the names come easier. There are five ranks: the Seven, the Emperor, the stars, the Harvest, and the Binding.

### The first rank: the Seven

The first seven months are the seven gods again, set down in the same order as the week, so the year opens with the whole god-house parade. If you know the days, you know these already.

| # | The month | For |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Monarchē** | Monas |
| 2 | **Theurion** | Theureus |
| 3 | **Wireon** | Firēs |
| 4 | **Barjon** | Barjas |
| 5 | **Phiron** | Phira |
| 6 | **Sophron** | Sophras |
| 7 | **Sounion** | Sounia |

Mark the snare in the third and the fifth. The wind-god Firēs and the love-goddess Phira are ever muddled on our frontier, for His name is spoken with a breath the ear takes for an F, and so **Wireon** and **Phiron** fall close together and are easily set one for the other. I have seen a whole village hold its wedding-fast a month early because a man could not tell the wind-month from the love-month. Keep the two apart in your writing, for the reeve will not forgive the error in a debt.

### The second rank: the Emperor's month

| # | The month | For |
|---|---|---|
| 8 | **Menneon** | Menneus, the Deathless Emperor |

One month for the immortal Emperor, and mark where they set it: just past the seven gods, in the eighth place, outside the god-row. He holds no day of the week, for he is no god; but he takes a month, for he is no common man. The Yona have a long word for what he is that I will not trouble you with. Call him the God-King's shadow and you will not go far wrong.

### The third rank: the old star-names

Now come nine months together, from the ninth to the seventeenth, and these are the strange ones, and I tell you plainly I do not know their meaning and neither, I think, do they.

| # | The month |
|---|---|
| 9 | **Verteon** |
| 10 | **Silvon** |
| 11 | **Taleon** |
| 12 | **Memron** |
| 13 | **Larikon** |
| 14 | **Limeon** |
| 15 | **Phornakon** |
| 16 | **Undon** |
| 17 | **Skepson** |

These nine are not gods, and not the Emperor, and not plain like the Harvest. They are older than the rest, and the priests who will preach a whole afternoon on Monas will go shut-mouthed when asked, only saying they are the old names for the stars. So a man learns them by rote and never asks again. When a Vestrner tells you he was born in Lark, he means Larikon, the thirteenth month. It is a name older than the Empire, and that is all any of us know.

### The fourth rank: the Harvest

| # | The month | For |
|---|---|---|
| 18 | **Hasaton** | the in-gathering |

This one comes not from the Yona tongue but from Doran, the folk of the Empire's own north, and it means the harvest, the "getting-in". Of all their months it is the honest one, for it names a thing a farmer knows well and not a god a priest keeps. Our folk take to it gladly, and here for once we are gracious the Empire gifted us something we can actually pronounce.

### The fifth rank: the Binding

| # | The month | For |
|---|---|---|
| 19 | **Desmia** | the binding, the chaining |

The last month is not a god, nor a harvest: it is a word, **binding**, the fetter, the chain. The Empire spends its whole nineteen days on it and calls it holy. I have been told, though I cannot swear it, that it is set against some old cast-out goddess, the loosing set against the binding, and that the year ends on her chaining every year on purpose, such that she may never be free of it. Whether that is so or a tale, mark this much: their year opens on a king and shuts on a chain. That tells you the shape of them better than any month I have named.

And mark one thing further, for it is the thing that should burn in you. What the Empire chains is always a woman. Their own cast-out goddess in this last month, and ours, Lysara, driven from the fields she kept. But of Lysara I will speak in her own place, which is nowhere in their calendar at all, and that absence is the whole of what I have left to teach you.

---

## The Gods We Kept, and the Gods They Gave Us

Before the Empire we kept the **Vanir**, with **Lysara** at their head, Lysara the **War-Maiden**, who rode before the host and chose who would fall and who would stand. She was ours as the dragons were, a thing grown of the land itself, older than any crown ever set upon a Vestrn brow. Her feasts were the hinges of our old year, and where you now see a Yona month stamped down you may be sure an old feast of hers lies buried under it, as a paved road lies over a grave.

Such is the power of the Severance. It reached into every land the Empire held and reshaped the heavens. **Vanirism**, the whole faith of the Vanir, was named unlawful in the Duchies. Lysara's altars burned to ash. To say her name over a field, as our grandmothers said it, is now a thing done low and quick, or not done at all.

And in the Vanirs' place now sit the **Aesir**, and the Empire calls it the true and rightful old faith of Vestrn, and the Aesir priests bless the imperial months and keep the reeve's holy days and teach in the schoolhouse that Lysara was a demon and the Vanir were never gods. Some of us kneel to them. A conquered folk will kneel to eat. But mark the trick of it, for it is the cleverest thing they have done to us: the Empire did not make Yona of us. It made us worship a Vestrn god against a Vestrn god, and set our own faith at our own throat, so the hand that holds us is our own. That is a subtler chain than any they laid on Lysara, and it is worn by men who believe themselves free.

---

## The Four Borrowed Days

Now I come to the four days left standing, that would not conform to the square, and this is the part that is ours more than theirs.

When the fifty-one weeks are spent and the nineteenth month is done, there come **four days that belong to no week and no god**. The Empire calls them the **Plerosis**, which in their high talk means a filling, a making-full, and they keep them as the crown of the year, four days of fires and forced feasting and a joy you had better be seen to share.

We call them the **Bordas**, the **borrowed days**, and we have called them so since before the reeve could stop us. For mark it: a filling fills a hole, and you do not fill what was never empty. Those four days are a gap, a thing left over, a debt the year owes. The Empire borrowed them to make its square shut, and a debt must one day be repayed.

The ancient elves of the forests, I am told, have their own word for the same four days, and their word also means a debt, a thing owed and let go. Two conquered folk, a world apart, who never spoke, both looked at those four days and saw a loan. Take that for what it is worth. I take it for a great deal.

And I will tell you what else those godless days are good for. When no imperial god rules the sky, an old god may walk in it. On the Bordas even the Aesir-priests fall quiet, for they will own those four days to none of their borrowed gods; and in that quiet, low and swift, the fields whisper Lysara again. The Empire made four days that belong to nothing, thinking little of what was there before.

---

## The Storm on the Bordas

I will end where our own hope ends, for you are old enough now to carry it.

The old wives hold that the Empire will fall upon a **Bordas that meets a great storm**. The Yona, when they hear us say it, think we mean their Barjas, their storm-judge, turning his bolt at the last upon his own masters. Let them think it. We mean no Yona god.

For the dragons are not dead. Mark above everything else I have set before you this day. The Empire took our crown, and threw down Lysara's altar, and named the Vanir demons, but it never killed the worms that sleep under the fells, for it could not; it only drove them deep, and set a watch on the deep places, and taught its children they were a story to frighten smaller children. But the land remembers what is its own. They lie coiled in the red dark beneath Vestrn, wing folded over wing, their fire banked and not gone out, and they are older than the Empire, and they will be here when it is blown sand.

And this is the storm we wait for. Not a judge's bolt, but a dragon's waking. One year, upon a day the Empire cannot own, upon a godless borrowed day when the Aesir priests stand dumb and Lysara's name is loud in the fields at last, the moors will crack and the long red sleep will end. Lysara will ride before them as she rode before our fathers, and the sky over Vestrn will burn as no Yona ever seen. On that day the debt of all the borrowed years will be called due, and shall be paid in fire. The square will not shut. And the Deathless, who cast out death itself and thought the reckoning closed, will learn the oldest lesson of our country: that a thing driven deep is not a thing destroyed, and a fire banked is not a fire extinguished.

I do not tell you this to set you down to wait. I have waited my whole life, and my father his before me, and we have seen the borrowed days come and go with no worm stirring under any hill. It may be a comfort and no more than a comfort. But you asked me for their calendar, and this is the last of it, and it is the only part the Empire could not give and cannot take from us: the four days a year not theirs, the goddess who walks in the silence of them, and the fire sleeping under our own feet, waiting for her call. Learn all the rest to keep from being cheated. Learn this to keep from despairing. And when you say the borrowed days aloud, say them low, and think of wings.

Now take up the reed, and we will write the nineteen months again, and this time you will name them to me.
