# Sideria

**Sideria** (from the Sylvan *sidera*, the fixed stars) was the faith of **[[Sylvargenta]]**, the silver-forest kingdom that stood before the desert. It is the oldest religion of which the Valle d'argento retains any record, and it is very nearly lost. What follows is reconstruction. Where the fragments fail, this article says so, for a false certainty would dishonour the dead worse than an honest gap.

Its object of worship was not a god who acts but a sky that holds: the **fixed stars**, the lights that do not wander. Its discipline was **stillness**. Its promise was not escape.

## A Note on the Sources

Nothing of Sideria survives whole. It comes to us in three broken forms, and this article is assembled from all three: the fragments kept in the Valle d'argento in modern **[[D'gento language|D'gento]]**; the parallel fragments of the **[[Ojapól]]** across the desert, who are the other half of us and kept other pieces; and the fossil-words that survive in neither liturgy nor scripture but in ordinary speech, carried so long that those who say them no longer hear what they mean.

The method of this study is to lay the D'gento fragment beside the Ojapól fragment and read what the two agree upon. Where they agree, we have something near the old faith. Where they differ, we have the shape of the wound that the desert cut between them. The reader should understand that to reconstruct Sideria at all is to perform, in miniature and on paper, the rejoining of a thing a god took the trouble to sever.

## The Fixed and the Flowing

The cosmos of Sideria has two kinds of thing in it, and everything else follows from the difference.

There are the things that **hold**, and there are the things that **move**.

The fixed stars hold. They do not wander as the sun and the moons and the five wanderers wander; they keep their stations through every night of every year, and a traveller who knows them may cross any sea or any forest in the dark and never be lost, because they are the one thing that will not move under him. To the Siderians this was not a convenience. It was the nearest thing the world offered to the holy: a fixed point, in a creation where almost nothing is fixed.

And there is the **[[River Nel|Nel]]**, which moves, and which is the river of souls, and which carries all of us. But here the old faith says a strange thing, and both halves of the tradition say it together, so we may trust it: **the Nel does not flow to anywhere.** It does not empty into a sea or climb to a god. It turns upon itself, across the planes of the world, and returns to where it began, and goes again, without end and without destination. The water that is a soul has been a soul before, and will be again, and the round of it does not close so much as *repeat*.

So the Siderian stood between two truths. He was carried on a current that goes nowhere and comes round again forever. And above him hung the only things that do not move.

**The whole of the faith is what he was told to do about that.**

## The Two Moons

The Siderian watched the things that hold, and reckoned the things that move to be beneath his notice: the sun, the two moons, the five wanderers that no fixed man would ever steer by. Yet even these the silver people named, as any people must name what it sees each night, and it is a small irony of our study that the names they gave the wanderers, which the faith held cheapest, have outlasted almost all it gave the stars it held dear. They were common coin. They lived in every mouth and not in the watchtowers alone, and common coin is worn slowly and lost last.

Of these the two moons are the surest recovery we possess, for here the sundered halves speak as near to one voice as the desert ever left them. In the Valle d'argento we call the greater moon **Luna Maggiore** and the lesser **Luna Sorella**; across the sand the [[Hoja plata]] say **Luna Mayor** and **Luna Sor**; and setting the one beside the other, the Sylvan root stands plain beneath both: **Luna Māior**, the greater, and **Luna Soror**, the sister. The greater is the slow one, that keeps a stately course. The sister is the swift, that will not rest but goes out and comes home and goes out again, and it is she the old word calls *soror*, the sister, though of whom, or of what, the fragments no longer say. That a people should give the restless light the name of kin, and keep the steady one at arm's length as merely the greater, is the kind of tenderness the old tongue is full of, and the kind of thing of which we recover a word, and a meaning, and never again the whole warmth.

## The Discipline of Stillness

He was told to hold still.

Not in the body only, though the Vigiles were known for a bodily stillness that unsettled outsiders. He was told to hold his *bearing*: to fix himself upon the things that do not move, to keep his orientation through the long turning of the river, and to **wait**.

For the doctrine of Sideria, as far as we can recover it, is a doctrine of **the right moment**. The Siderian does not chase. He does not strive against the current, which cannot be strived against, and he does not try to climb out of it, which the orthodox held could not be done. He orients, and he holds, and he waits for the moment to come to him; and because the river returns upon itself, the moment he waits for is a moment that **comes around again**. To lose your bearing is the only true failure, for a man still oriented will know his moment when it arrives, and a man who has drifted will let it pass without ever seeing it.

This is why they watched the sky. Not to predict, as the astronomers of the empire predict, and not to worship a power that might be begged. They watched to **keep their place**. The fixed stars were the instrument by which a soul on an endless river held still.

Their watchers were the **Vigiles**, and their long observance was the **Vigilia**, the watch; and of what the Vigilia consisted we know less than we would wish. That effigies were set at fixed stations and given at last to the waters of the Nel seems certain, for both halves remember it. What was said over them, and what was waited for, the fragments do not agree.

Here the tradition divides, and honestly. Some fragments speak as though the awaited moment were an **alignment that returns each cycle**, so that the faithful soul catches it as a sailor catches a tide. Others speak as though it were a thing that **had not yet come at all**, in any turning of the river, and that a star would announce it when it did. A few, which the pious of both halves treated as suspect, speak of a moment at which a soul might **leave the river altogether**, stepping off the endless round, once, at exactly the right instant, into we are not told what. Whether these are three doctrines, or three misreadings of one, cannot now be determined.

## The Dii, or the Court of the Stars

The fixed figures were the **[[The Dii|Dii]]** (singular *Dius*): nine constellations, the standing court of the unmoving sky. And here, for once, we are not poor. This is the best-preserved matter in all of Sideria, because it was never only a matter of religion. **The nine Dii were the government of Sylvargenta**, and a people remembers its constitution longer than it remembers its prayers.

The word itself is worth a moment, for it is a fossil that does not sit quietly. In the old tongue *Dii* means, plainly, **gods**. Yet whether the Siderians *meant* it so, or whether the figures were rather their ancestors and magistrates raised into the sky and only loosely called divine, is exactly what the fragments will not settle. The reader should hold the tension rather than resolve it, for the truth may be that the Siderians held it unresolved themselves.

For **Sylvargenta was a federation**: eight **groves**, each a wood and a people and a seat, and one **capital**, **[[Verta]]**, from which the whole was arbitrated. They ruled not by a king and not by a vote quickly taken, but by **debate**, long and unhurried, patient beyond the patience of any mortalkin, for time was the one thing the silver people never lacked and never spent carelessly. To sit, to hear every side, to turn a question over across a season or a life, was not their means of government. It was their government.

And they set that council in the sky. The nine Dii are the nine seats, fixed overhead, arguing forever. This is why one steers by them: they are not powers who act, but **positions that hold**, the whole spread of what may be said about any matter, standing still and available in the dark, so that a soul lost on the turning river might find, in the fixed council above, every voice it needed to hear.

### The Nine

At the head sits **[[Vertex]]**, the Zenith, whose seat is the capital [[Verta]]. Vertex is the pole, the still point about which the whole sky turns and which does not itself turn; and in the council Vertex was the **arbiter**, who holds no position of its own and exists so that the other eight may be heard against one another. That the capital's name and the pole's name are one word is not coincidence but doctrine: the still centre of the realm and the still centre of the sky were understood to be the same office.

The eight groves, as the two halves between them allow us to recover, were these:

- **Silva**, the Wood: the conservative voice of the forest itself, speaking for the standing trees and what was already grown.
- **Talea**, the Cutting: the voice of new growth, of planting and revival and the spreading of the wood into new ground. Silva and Talea are remembered as **rivals of long standing**, the old timber against the new slip, and much of the Court's business was the quarrel between them.
- **Memor**, the Precedent: the keeper of what once was, in whose grove stood the **great library** of the realm.
- **Larix**, the Hearth: the household voice, the warmth of the kindred; and here the fragments are pointed, for Larix was the god of the **silver people specifically**, of their own blood and hearth, and not of outsiders.
- **Limes**, the Border: the outward grove, of the frontier and the muster and the dealing with foreign powers, whose imagery is of the **shore and the sea**, the edge where the wood met the wider world.
- **Fornax**, the Kiln: the makers, the crafters, the artisans and their fires.
- **Unda**, the Flowing: the waters: the rivers and lakes of the living land and their keeping, and also, and not separately in the Siderian mind, the great river of stars.
- **Scepsis**, the Doubt: the scholars, whose charge was to reopen what the others thought settled, and to whom, the fragments quietly indicate, the realm also entrusted its **watchers and its secrets**.

### The Empty Seats

Here the Court's long peace breaks, and the break is still visible overhead.

When the forest burned, three groves burned with it, wholly and past recovery: **Silvátra** the seat of Silva, **Undana** the seat of Unda, and **Limeta** the seat of Limes. Their lands are ash and desert now; their peoples are scattered or dead. **And yet their seats remain.** The Court is still a court of nine, because the nine are fixed in the sky and the sky did not burn; the figures of the Wood and the Flowing and the Border still stand among the stars exactly where they always stood, as though nothing had happened to the groves below them.

Someone must speak for an empty chair. And it is **Verta** that speaks for them, the capital filling the seats of the dead groves with its own voices, so that the arbiter who once held no position of its own now holds, in effect, four. Whether this was a mercy, a usurpation, or merely the only thing that could be done, the descendants of the silver people have never agreed, and the Valle d'argento and the Hoja plata do not agree now. It is the oldest live grievance we possess: that the still centre, by the accident of catastrophe, became the thing that speaks loudest. The sky shows a council of nine in perfect balance. The history shows four of those voices coming from one mouth.

### The Question of Their Nature

Whether the Siderians took the Dii to be **gods** in earnest, powers with wills to be petitioned, or **the remembered dead**, magistrates and founders raised into the fixed sky and honoured there, is a question this study cannot close, though the very name would seem to answer it. A council is a thing of persons; a constellation is a thing of lights; and the Dii were somehow both, worshipped and consulted at once, in a way the sundered fragments preserve only in pieces. That a people might set its own parliament in heaven and then kneel to it is strange to us. It does not seem to have been strange to them.

## The Zeroth: Vacuus

There was a schism, and it was about **nothing**, which is to say, about whether nothing is a thing.

The orthodox counted nine Dii, and held the darkness between and behind them to be simple **absence**: the night is where no star is, and that is all the night is. But there were those who counted differently. They said that before the nine there was a **tenth**, or rather a *first*, which was not a light but the **dark itself**, the black upon which all the stars are set, which was there before any of them and would remain when they had gone. They called it **[[Vacuus]]**, which in the old tongue is *the empty*, and they named it the one before all.

To the orthodox this was worse than error; it was a kind of blasphemy of counting, for it gave a name and a rank to what the faithful had always regarded as merely the space left over. To the schismatics it was the most obvious truth in the sky, and the orthodox were the blind ones: how could the lights be first, when the dark they hang in must already have been there to hang them?

The dispute cannot be settled from the fragments and perhaps could not be settled at all, being less a question of fact than of how one is disposed to see. But it is a curious thing, and this study records it without pretending to understand it, that the Siderians of both parties agreed the matter was grave. A people that watched the fixed lights to keep from being lost did not treat lightly the suggestion that behind the lights there was a older thing, unlit, that had been waiting longer than any of them.

## Sylvargenta and the Sundering

All of this belonged to **[[Sylvargenta]]**, the silver forest: one kingdom, one wood, one people, in an age so far behind us that the empire which now overshadows the world was not yet imagined. The name means the silver forest, and the two lands that remember it kept its meaning between them, each holding half: the **[[Valle d'argento]]**, the valley of silver, and the **[[Hoja plata]]**, the silver leaf. We are the halves of a broken word.

What broke it was not war and not time. **A god blighted the land**, and where the forest had been there came the **[[Great Desert of Wei]]**, and the wood that had joined the silver people to one another was replaced by a waste that no one crosses lightly to this day. The kingdom did not fall so much as it was *cut*, and the two edges of the cut drew apart down the centuries into two peoples who no longer share a tongue and had, until scholarship began its slow rejoining, half-forgotten they were ever one.

Which god, and why, this study will not pretend to know. The fragments name the blight but they do not agree upon the hand behind it, and this is not a gap a careful reader will want filled with a guess. It is enough, and it is grief enough, to say that the desert the empire's armies later crossed to make their western war was once the living heart of a kingdom, and that it was made a desert on purpose, by something that could.

## What Became of the Faith

Sideria did not survive the Sundering whole, and it does not survive now as a living worship, or not visibly, or not in any form its ancient keepers would recognise. The stars it watched are still overhead, unmoved, indifferent to whether anyone below still keeps the watch; and their names persist in the mouths of the Valle d'argento and the Hoja plata as words for places and seasons and directions, emptied of the faith that first spoke them, in the way that a people will go on saying *by the pole* long after they have forgotten there was ever a god in it.

It is the particular sorrow of the antiquary that we recover most easily what mattered least: a direction-word, a fixed-star name, the shape of a rite. What the Siderian *felt*, holding still on the endless river with his eyes on the one thing that would not move, waiting through the whole turning of the world for a moment he trusted would come around again: that we reconstruct only by inference, and hold only for a moment, and cannot keep.
